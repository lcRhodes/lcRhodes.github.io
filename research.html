Correlated electron materials

Correlated electron materials are systems where electron-electron interactions generate novel ground states, such as superconductivity and exotic magnetic ordering. They often occur when no single energy scale dominates and thus become difficult to describe using conventional theoretical methods, analogous to the challenge of simulating fluid dynamics in classical mechanics.

My work focuses on understanding the low energy electronic structure of correlated electron materials using a combination of density functional theory, tight binding modelling, Angle Resolved Photoemission Spectroscopy and Scanning Tunnelling Microscopy. By developing these quantitatively accurate models, constrained against experimental data, I am able to identify the phenomenology of the correlated ground states on the electronic structure and therefore constrain and refine possible theoretical explanations as to the origin of the novel ground states of real materials.

Enhancing the critical temperature of unconventional superconductors

Superconductivity, the ability to carry electric currents with zero resistance and loss, has the potential to radically transform our capacity to generate, transport and use electrical power. The main impediment is the very low temperatures (approximately -250 degrees Celsius) required to reach this state. The few superconductors which have this property at substantially higher temperatures, the so-called high-temperature superconductors, are still poorly understood and the mystery behind why these materials become superconducting remains one of the largest unsolved problems in condensed matter physics. Solving this puzzle promises new routes to design superconductors with higher transition temperatures or tailored properties for specific applications.

My current work focuses on understanding spin-fluctuation mediated superconductivity, thought to be important in many correlated electron materials that exhibit the largest superconducting transition temperatures. I develop accurate electronic structure models of correlated electron materials and use the latest advancements in superconducting theory techniques, such as functional renormalisation group theory, to study how superconductivity, magnetism and charge ordering compete and coexist assuming dominant spin fluctuation scenario. By comparing these predictions with experimental data from collaborators, we are then able to deepen our understanding of spin-fluctuations in these materials and predict and confirm new pathways to enhance the superconducting properties of real correlated electron materials.

Novel ordering at surfaces and interfaces

The surface of a material is a unique environment where the translational symmetry is broken and the system experiences an additional surface potential, in correlated electron materials this potential can be strong enough to stabilise novel local electronic states and ordering, as well as topologically protected surface states. For example, using scanning tunnelling microscopy and theoretical modelling, Masahiro, Izidor, Peter and I were able to identify that the non-magnetic material Sr3Ru2O7 actually exhibits 2D in-plane ferromagnetism at the surface. Surfaces and interfaces are a rich ground for identifying new forms of correlated electronic states, however experimental detection is challenging. This is why I have been developing numerical methods to model surfaces and interfaces to enable us to uncover the novel ground states being displayed at the surfaces of correlated materials.
